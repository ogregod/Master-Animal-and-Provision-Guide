# NEVERWINTER NORTHERN PIG
## Cold-Hardy Lard Production Specialist

*In The North, winter means survival. And survival means lard. Neverwinter Northern Pigs aren't raised primarily for meat - they're raised for the 28 pounds of premium white lard that keeps families alive through six months of brutal cold. Cooking fat, heating fuel, candle light, soap - all from one pig's fat. The meat is good, yes. But the lard? The lard is essential.*

**— Thadius Del Irontet, Master Butcher**

---

## QUICK STATS

**Primary Purpose:** Lard Production (winter survival essential)

**Secondary Purpose:** Fresh pork, smoked/cured products

**Origin:** Neverwinter region, bred for cold tolerance

**Climate Adaptation:** Cold-hardy, winter specialists (-20°F tolerance)

**Slaughter Age:** 8-9 months at optimal weight (cold slows growth)

## PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS

### **Size & Weight**

**Processing Standard:** 235 lb pig at 8-9 months

**Live Weight by Season:**
- **Spring farrowing** (optimal): 220-250 lbs by late fall (full growth season)
- **Early summer farrowing**: 180-220 lbs by late fall (shortened season, acceptable)
- **Late summer/fall farrowing**: Not viable (insufficient time before winter)

**Growth Rate:** ~0.85 lbs/day overall (slower than temperate breeds due to cold, energy spent on warmth)

**Key Difference from Baseline:** -15 lbs lighter (cold climate = slower growth), but **+2% body fat** (cold adaptation = thick insulating layer).

**Critical Slaughter Timing:**
Northern pigs MUST be slaughtered before deep winter (Sept-Oct optimal). Once temperatures drop below 0°F consistently, outdoor processing impossible. This creates compressed slaughter season (6-8 week window).

### **Appearance**

**Color:**
- White/pink skin with dense coat (60%)
- Black with thick bristles (25%)
- Red/brown with heavy winter coat (15%)

**Build:**
- **Compact, stocky body** (cold adaptation - reduced surface area)
- **Thick subcutaneous fat layer** (visible, cold insulation)
- **Short legs** (less heat loss)
- **Dense winter coat** (long bristles, underfur development)
- **Small ears** (frostbite prevention)

**Distinctive Features:**
- Visibly fat (rounder body shape - not obesity, adaptation)
- Heavy jowls (fat storage)
- Thick bristles (winter coat protection)
- Small ears (cold protection - compare to large ears in warm climates)

**Height:** 26-30 inches at shoulder (shorter than baseline)

**Winter Adaptation:**
- Subcutaneous fat: 2-3 inches thick (baseline: 1-1.5 inches)
- Can survive -20°F outdoor temperatures (brief periods)
- Snow foraging ability (root through light snow for food)
- Group huddling behavior (social warmth)

### **Temperament**

**Disposition:** Calm, hardy, survival-focused

- Cold-adapted (unfazed by winter weather)
- Less active in cold (energy conservation)
- Social/group-oriented (huddling for warmth)
- Efficient feeders (maximizing calorie intake for fat storage)

**Processing Note:** Calm temperament critical in harsh climate (stress during processing in cold = meat quality issues). Northern pigs evolved for low-stress slaughter.


**Complete Breakdown:**
```

- Premium (loin, tenderloin): 29 lbs
- Choice (shoulder, ham fresh): 25 lbs
- Standard (belly, ribs): 28 lbs
- Utility (ground, trim): 6 lbs

BYPRODUCTS (LARD IS THE KEY):
+ Lard: 28 lbs (12% live weight vs 10% baseline) ⭐ @ 5-6 sp/lb = 140-168 sp
+ Hide: 14 lbs (6%) @ 0.8 sp/lb = 11.2 sp (thick, but small)
+ Organs: 7.5 lbs (3.2%) @ various = 4 sp

```

**Critical Insight:**
Retail meat: 88 lbs @ ~2 sp/lb avg = 176 sp
Lard: 28 lbs @ 5.5 sp/lb avg = **154 sp**

**LARD VALUE NEARLY EQUALS MEAT VALUE.**

In deep winter (Dec-Feb), when lard sells for 6-7 sp/lb (premium demand), lard value (168-196 sp) EXCEEDS meat value. This is why Northern pigs are bred for fat, not lean meat.

### **Primal Distribution (Compared to Baseline)**

- **Loin:** 17% → ~28 lbs retail (slightly smaller - less priority)
- **Ham:** 19% → ~32 lbs retail (slightly smaller)
- **Belly:** 13% → ~22 lbs retail (+1% over baseline - more fat layer)
- **Boston Butt:** 8% → ~13 lbs retail (standard)
- **Picnic Shoulder:** 8% → ~13 lbs retail (standard)
- **Spareribs:** 5% → ~8 lbs retail (standard)
- **Head:** 8% → ~13 lbs (standard)
- **Jowl:** 4% → ~7 lbs (+1% - fat accumulation)
- **Feet:** 4% → ~6 lbs (standard)

**Fat Distribution Emphasis:**
Cold-adapted pigs store fat:
1. **Subcutaneous** (under skin, back fat): 60% of total fat (insulation)
2. **Internal** (leaf lard, kidney fat): 30% (organ protection)
3. **Intramuscular** (marbling): 10% (less than warm-climate pigs)

This creates EXCELLENT lard yields (large, thick deposits) but less marbling in meat (leaner cuts, tougher without proper cooking).

### **Processing Time**

**Total Active Labor:** 10 hours average

**Timeline:**
- Initial processing: 1.5-2 hours (cold weather = faster work to prevent freezing)
- Chilling: 12-18 hours at 30-35°F (natural cold - outdoor hanging)
- Primal breakdown: 1.5-2 hours
- Retail fabrication: 5-6 hours (standard)

**Labor Cost:** ~40 sp (10 hours × 4 sp/hour journeyman rate)

**Cold Weather Processing:**
- **Advantage:** Natural refrigeration (Sept-Nov ambient 30-40°F)
- **Challenge:** Must complete before deep freeze (once <20°F, outdoor work dangerous)
- **Community approach:** Neighborhood cooperatives (process 5-10 pigs in 2-3 days, shared labor/warmth)

## LARD PRODUCTION (PRIMARY PURPOSE)

### **NORTHERN LARD - Winter Survival Essential**

**Why Neverwinter Northern Pig Excels at Lard:**

1. **Yield:** 28 lbs lard (vs 25 lbs baseline) = +12% production
2. **Quality:** Premium white, firm texture (cold adaptation)
3. **Fat Thickness:** 2-3 inch back fat layer (vs 1-1.5 baseline)
4. **Storage:** Cold climate = natural preservation (lard keeps 12+ months without refrigeration)
5. **Necessity:** Winter survival (not luxury - essential)

**Lard Rendering (Northern Method):**

**Process:**
1. **Separate by quality:**
   - Leaf lard (premium, internal)
   - Back fat (primary volume, subcutaneous)
   - Trim fat (standard quality)

2. **Cut into small pieces** (1-2 inch cubes - increases surface area)

3. **Render (low heat, 8-12 hours):**
   - Large pot or rendering kettle
   - Low heat (200-220°F) - slow rendering preserves quality
   - Stir occasionally (prevent burning)
   - Fat melts, cracklings float

4. **Strain:**
   - Remove cracklins (fried fat solids - edible snack)
   - Strain through cheesecloth (remove impurities)
   - Cool in crocks/tins

5. **Storage:**
   - Cool, dry location (naturally cold in North - 30-40°F)
   - Sealed containers (prevent oxidation)
   - Lasts 12-18 months in northern climate

**Time Investment:** 3-4 hours active labor + 8-12 hours rendering

**Yield:** 28 lbs raw fat → 26 lbs rendered lard + 2 lbs cracklins (7% loss to moisture/impurities)

**See Master Butcher's Guide § Preservation Methods, Lard Rendering, Cold Climate Methods**

**Economic Reality:**
A northern household uses 20-30 lbs lard per winter (Oct-March). One pig's lard (28 lbs) sustains a family for one winter. This is why **every family raises 1-2 pigs minimum** - lard is non-negotiable survival requirement.

## MEAT PRESERVATION (Secondary Focus)

Northern climate allows preservation methods difficult elsewhere.

### **SMOKED HAM (Primary Meat Preservation)**

**Why Smoking (Not Long-Cure Aging):**
- Cold climate = extended smoking possible (Sept-Nov ambient 40-50°F ideal)
- Aging facilities rare (no controlled environment homesteads)
- Smoking = robust preservation (6-8 months storage)
- Cultural tradition (northern smokehouse architecture)

**Northern Smoked Ham Process:**

**Quick Cure:**
1. Injection brine (salt, sugar, Prague Powder, water) - 2 days
2. Dry rub (salt, pepper, herbs) - 5 days
3. Rinse and dry - 12 hours

**Cold Smoking (Extended):**
4. Smoke at 65-85°F (cool climate advantage)
5. 24-48 hours total smoking time (heavy smoke)
6. Wood: Local varieties (birch, alder, occasional oak if available)

**Yields:**
- 26 lbs fresh ham → 21 lbs smoked ham (19% moisture loss)
- 2 hams (10.5 lbs each)

**Value:**
- Fresh ham: 26 lbs @ 2 sp/lb = 52 sp
- **Smoked ham:** 21 lbs @ 4.5-5 sp/lb = 94.5-105 sp
- **VALUE INCREASE: +82-102%** (42.5-53 sp profit)

**Storage:** 6-8 months hung in cool, dry location (natural northern climate)

**See Master Butcher's Guide § Preservation Methods, Cold Smoking Techniques**

### **SAUSAGE PRODUCTION (Volume Preservation)**

**Boston Butt + Trim → Smoked Sausage:**

**Northern Sausage Characteristics:**
- Higher sage content (regional preference - available wild herb)
- Smoked (preservation + flavor)
- Coarse grind (traditional texture)
- 30/70 fat/lean ratio (add back some lard for moisture in lean northern pork)

**Process:**
1. Grind Boston Butt (13 lbs) + add 3-4 lbs lard (lean pork needs fat supplement)
2. Season with Northern Sage blend (see Daggerford Farm Pig - Northern Sage)
3. Stuff into casings
4. Cold smoke 24-36 hours (preservation)

**Yields:**
- 13 lbs Boston Butt + 4 lbs added lard = 17 lbs sausage mix
- Smoked: 15 lbs smoked sausage (12% moisture loss)

**Value:**
- Fresh Boston Butt: 13 lbs @ 2.2 sp/lb = 28.6 sp
- Added lard cost: 4 lbs @ 5 sp/lb = 20 sp (internal cost)
- **Smoked sausage:** 15 lbs @ 3.5 sp/lb = 52.5 sp
- Net value gain: 52.5 - 28.6 = 23.9 sp (before lard cost)
- **Actual profit:** 3.9 sp (low, but extends storage to 4-6 months)

**Note:** Sausage is preservation strategy, not profit center. Goal is winter food storage, not revenue maximization.

## CULTURAL NOTES

### **Northern Survival Economics**

**Lard as Currency:**

In northern settlements (Neverwinter region, Ten-Towns), lard functions as **informal currency** during winter:

**Barter Exchange Rates (winter):**
- 1 lb premium lard = 5-6 sp value (or trade goods equivalent)
- Lard trades for: firewood, grain, preserved fish, candles, soap supplies
- "Lard-rich" families have economic power (can trade for necessities)

**Why Lard is Valuable:**
1. **Multi-use:** Cooking, heating, lighting, soap, leather - one product, many needs
2. **Storage:** Keeps 12-18 months without refrigeration (long-term wealth)
3. **Portable:** 28 lbs lard = compact, transportable (vs 200 lbs firewood for equivalent heating)
4. **Scarcity:** Winter production impossible (fall slaughter only)
5. **Necessity:** Not luxury - survival requirement

**Social Status:**
"Fat pig families" = respected (literal and metaphorical wealth). Raising 3-5 pigs/year = prosperity signal (lard surplus = can trade, not just subsist).

### **Fall Slaughter Festivals (Northern Tradition)**

**Community Cooperation:**

Northern slaughter is **mandatory community event** (not optional cooperation like Heartlands):

**Process:**
1. **Timing coordination:** All families slaughter same 2-week window (Sept-Oct, before freeze)
2. **Labor rotation:** 5-10 families assist each family (1-2 days per household)
3. **Shared equipment:** Scalding tubs, rendering kettles, smokehouses (expensive, communally owned)
4. **Skill transmission:** Elders teach youth (survival skill = life-or-death knowledge)

**Celebration Aspects:**
- Fresh pork feast (organs, variety cuts - celebratory meal before preservation begins)
- Lard rendering as social event (12-hour process = conversation, storytelling)
- First sausage tasting (quality evaluation, recipe sharing)
- Children's participation (learning butchering/rendering = survival education)

**Economic Reality:**
Cooperation isn't kindness - it's survival necessity. No family can process 5-10 pigs alone in 2-week window. Cooperation = everyone survives winter.

## BREEDING NOTES (For Northern Farmers)

**Cold-Adapted Selection Criteria:**

**Fat Development (Primary):**
- Thick subcutaneous layer (visual assessment - rounder pigs preferred)
- Back fat measurement (2-3 inches ideal)
- Lard yield (post-slaughter family records - select offspring of high-yielding sows)

**Cold Tolerance (Secondary):**
- Small ears (frostbite resistance)
- Dense coat (winter bristle development)
- Activity in cold (pigs that stay active in snow = hardy genetics)
- Survival rate (pigs that survive northern winters without shelter loss)

**Calm Temperament (Tertiary):**
- Low-stress handling (northern processing in cold = stress management critical)
- Group compatibility (winter huddling behavior = survival advantage)

**Breeding Program:**
Northern farmers maintain **regional gene pool** (closed community, occasional Heartlands cross for hybrid vigor). Selection is **functional, not aesthetic** - survival traits prioritized over appearance.

**Breeding Stock Availability:**
- Gilts (young females): 4-5 sp/lb (940-1,175 sp for 235 lb gilt)
- Purpose: Establish/maintain northern herd
- Source: Local farmers (community-based breeding)

**Cross-Breeding:**
Occasional Daggerford Farm Pig cross (imported gilt) = larger offspring, but:
- First generation: Good (hybrid vigor)
- Second generation: Less cold-adapted (lose northern traits)
- **Solution:** Continuous backcrossing to Northern genetics (maintain cold tolerance)
