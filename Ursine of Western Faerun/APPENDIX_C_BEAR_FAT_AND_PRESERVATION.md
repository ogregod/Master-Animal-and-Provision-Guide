## I. BEAR FAT RENDERING MASTERY

### Why Bear Fat Rendering is Critical

**Bear fat rendering is often 30-50% of total bear value.** A 300 lb black bear yields 25 lbs of rendered grease worth 125 sp (12.5 gp). That's more than the backstrap steaks, ribs, and hams COMBINED. Skip rendering and you lose 35-40% of potential profit.

**Economics:**
- Fresh fat trim: 1-2 sp/lb (30-60 sp for 30 lbs)
- Rendered bear grease: 5-6 sp/lb (125-150 sp for 25 lbs)
- **Value increase: +200-300%**

---

### Fat Types & Quality Grading

Not all bear fat is equal. Location on the bear determines quality.

**LEAF FAT (HIGHEST QUALITY):**
- **Location:** Around kidneys, heart, inside body cavity
- **Characteristics:** Pure white, firm, minimal blood vessels
- **Rendered Color:** Crystal clear to pale yellow
- **Flavor:** Mildest, almost neutral
- **Smoke Point:** 375°F (highest)
- **Uses:** Baking (pie crusts), fine cooking, medicinal (purest)
- **Yield:** 3-5 lbs per bear (5-8% of total fat)
- **Price:** 6-8 sp/lb (premium quality)

**BACK FAT (STANDARD QUALITY):**
- **Location:** Thick layer along spine, shoulders, rump
- **Characteristics:** White to cream, some blood vessels
- **Rendered Color:** Light yellow to golden
- **Flavor:** Mild, slightly gamey
- **Smoke Point:** 350-360°F (standard)
- **Uses:** General cooking, frying, sautéing
- **Yield:** 15-20 lbs per bear (60-70% of total fat)
- **Price:** 5-6 sp/lb (standard quality)

**TRIM FAT (UTILITY QUALITY):**
- **Location:** Mixed sources (belly, between muscles, around organs)
- **Characteristics:** Yellow to cream, blood vessels, connective tissue
- **Rendered Color:** Golden to amber
- **Flavor:** Gamey, stronger
- **Smoke Point:** 325-340°F (lower)
- **Uses:** Waterproofing, soap/candle making, medicinal rubs
- **Yield:** 7-10 lbs per bear (25-35% of total fat)
- **Price:** 3-4 sp/lb (utility quality)

### WET RENDERING (Recommended for Beginners)

**Process:** Render fat in water to prevent scorching.

**Equipment:**
- Large pot (cast iron or heavy copper)
- Water (enough to cover fat)
- Long wooden spoon
- Strainer (fine mesh or cheesecloth)
- Heat source (hearth, stove, outdoor fire)
- Ceramic crocks for storage

**Steps:**

**1. Prepare Fat:**
- Cube all fat into 1-inch pieces (smaller = faster rendering)
- Separate leaf fat from back fat from trim (render separately for quality grading)
- Remove any blood, meat, or connective tissue

**2. Start Rendering:**
- Place cubed fat in pot
- Add water (1 cup per 5 lbs fat)
- Heat slowly to 180-200°F (do NOT boil)

**3. Simmer:**
- Maintain 180-200°F for 6-8 hours
- Stir occasionally (every 30-60 minutes)
- Fat will melt, water will evaporate
- **Visual Cue:** Fat cubes shrink, turn golden/brown (cracklings)

**4. Test for Completion:**
- Fat should be clear/translucent (not cloudy)
- Cracklings float on surface and are golden/crispy
- **Test:** Drop of grease on cool plate should solidify within 30 seconds

**5. Strain:**
- Remove from heat
- Strain through fine mesh or 4 layers of cheesecloth
- **SAVE CRACKLINGS** (bonus food product, 1 sp/lb)

**6. Cool & Store:**
- Pour liquid grease into ceramic crocks
- Cool slowly (room temperature, 2-4 hours)
- Grease will solidify as it cools
- Seal with tight lid or wax paper

**Yield:** 30 lbs fat trim → 25 lbs rendered grease (83% yield)

**Time:** 8-10 hours total (mostly unattended simmering)

**Advantages:**
- Hard to scorch (water prevents burning)
- Forgiving for beginners
- Clean flavor (impurities sink in water)

**Disadvantages:**
- Slightly lower quality (water contact)
- Longer time (evaporation required)

---

### DRY RENDERING (Professional Method)

**Process:** Render fat with NO water, direct heat control.

**Equipment:**
- Heavy pot (cast iron preferred)
- Low, steady heat source
- Wooden spoon
- Strainer
- Ceramic crocks

**Steps:**

**1. Prepare Fat:** (same as wet rendering)

**2. Start Rendering:**
- Place cubed fat in pot (NO WATER)
- Start with VERY LOW heat
- Fat will release its own liquid as it melts

**3. Render:**
- Maintain LOW heat (160-180°F)
- Stir frequently (every 15-20 minutes)
- **CRITICAL:** Watch for scorching (burnt smell = ruined batch)
- Fat will bubble gently

**4. Completion:**
- Cracklings turn golden and crispy
- Liquid grease is clear and amber
- Minimal bubbling

**5. Strain & Store:** (same as wet rendering)

**Yield:** 30 lbs fat trim → 26 lbs rendered grease (87% yield - higher than wet)

**Time:** 5-7 hours (requires more attention)

**Advantages:**
- **Higher quality** (purest flavor, clearest grease)
- **Higher yield** (+4% over wet rendering)
- Faster (no water to evaporate)

**Disadvantages:**
- Easy to scorch (requires experience)
- Must monitor closely (can't leave unattended)
- Burnt batch = total loss

**Professional Recommendation:** Use wet rendering for trim fat, dry rendering for leaf fat only.

---

### Cracklings Production

**Cracklings are the crispy, fried fat cubes left after rendering. They're edible and valuable.**

**After Straining Grease:**
1. Spread cracklings on flat surface
2. Season with salt (optional: pepper, herbs)
3. Let cool and dry (1-2 hours)
4. Store in dry container

**Uses:**
- Snack food (crunchy, savory, high calorie)
- Cooking fat supplement (add back to stews for flavor)
- Grinding into cooking fat powder

**Value:** 1 sp/lb (bonus product)

**Yield:** 25 lbs fat → 3-4 lbs cracklings (12-15% of fat weight)

**Storage:** 2-4 weeks (dry, cool conditions)

---

### Storage & Shelf Life

**Proper storage prevents rancidity (fat spoilage).**

**Containers:**
- **Best:** Ceramic crocks with tight lids (air-tight seal)
- **Good:** Glass jars with wax seals
- **Avoid:** Wood barrels (absorbs fat, hard to clean), metal (can react with fat)

**Storage Conditions:**
- **Temperature:** Cool (40-60°F ideal), dark location
- **Light:** Avoid direct sunlight (light causes rancidity)
- **Air:** Minimize air contact (use full containers, tight seals)

**Shelf Life:**
- **Room temperature (60-70°F):** 3-6 months
- **Cool cellar (40-50°F):** 6-12 months
- **Frozen (below 32°F):** 12-24 months

**Signs of Rancidity:**
- Off smell (sour, bitter, chemical)
- Color change (darkening, yellow to brown)
- Taste change (bitter, unpleasant)
- **If rancid:** Discard (not safe for consumption)

---

### Troubleshooting Common Problems

**PROBLEM: Grease is Cloudy/Milky**
- **Cause:** Water or impurities not fully removed
- **Fix:** Re-heat grease to 200°F, let settle 1 hour, carefully pour off clear grease (leave sediment)

**PROBLEM: Grease Smells Burnt**
- **Cause:** Temperature too high, scorching
- **Fix:** Cannot fix burnt grease - discard, start over

**PROBLEM: Grease Won't Solidify**
- **Cause:** Under-rendered (still contains water or impurities)
- **Fix:** Re-heat, continue rendering until clear

**PROBLEM: Cracklings are Soggy**
- **Cause:** Removed too early, not fully crisped
- **Fix:** Return to pot, continue rendering until crispy

**PROBLEM: Low Yield (Less than 80%)**
- **Cause:** Fat trim left meat/connective tissue on, or rendering incomplete
- **Fix:** Clean fat better before rendering, render longer

## II. BEAR GREASE APPLICATIONS

### Cooking Uses

**Bear grease is a premium cooking fat with high smoke point and neutral flavor (when properly rendered).**

**Smoke Point Comparison:**
- Bear grease (back fat): 350-360°F
- Bear grease (leaf fat): 375°F
- Butter: 300-350°F
- Lard (pork): 370-375°F
- Tallow (beef): 400-420°F

**Best Cooking Applications:**
- **Frying:** High smoke point, crispy results (fried potatoes, meat)
- **Sautéing:** Neutral flavor, doesn't overpower delicate dishes
- **Baking:** Leaf fat in pie crusts (flaky, tender results)
- **Roasting:** Baste meats for moisture and flavor

**Flavor Profile:**
- Leaf fat: Almost neutral (mild, clean)
- Back fat: Slightly gamey (rich, savory)
- Trim fat: Gamey (strong, best for hearty dishes)

**Storage for Cooking:** Keep small jar (1-2 lbs) in kitchen, bulk storage in cellar

---

### Waterproofing Leather

**Bear grease is traditionally used to waterproof boots, gloves, belts, and leather armor.**

**Formula: Basic Leather Waterproofing**

**Ingredients:**
- 1 lb rendered bear grease (back fat or trim fat)
- 2 oz beeswax (optional, for firmer finish)
- 1 oz pine resin (optional, for extra water resistance)

**Process:**
1. Melt bear grease in pot (low heat)
2. Add beeswax and pine resin (if using), stir until dissolved
3. Remove from heat, let cool slightly
4. Apply to CLEAN, DRY leather while still warm
5. Rub in thoroughly with cloth
6. Let dry 24 hours
7. Buff with clean cloth

**Application Rate:** 1 lb waterproofing treats:
- 4-6 pairs of boots
- 2-3 leather armor sets
- 8-10 belts or gloves

**Results:**
- Water beads on surface (not absorbed)
- Leather stays supple (doesn't dry/crack)
- Lasts 3-6 months (reapply as needed)

**Economic Value:** 1 lb waterproofing grease = 8-12 sp (premium over cooking grease)

**Market:** Leatherworkers, adventurers, soldiers, frontier settlers

### Cold Protection Balm

**Bear grease prevents frostbite and protects skin in extreme cold. ESSENTIAL in Icewind Dale.**

**Formula: Frostbite Prevention Balm**

**Ingredients:**
- 8 oz rendered bear grease (leaf fat preferred)
- 2 oz beeswax (for consistency)
- 1 oz wintergreen oil (optional, for scent and minor pain relief)
- 1/2 oz camphor (optional, warming sensation)

**Process:**
1. Melt bear grease and beeswax together (low heat)
2. Remove from heat
3. Add wintergreen and camphor (if using), stir
4. Pour into small tins or jars
5. Let cool and solidify

**Application:**
- Apply thin layer to exposed skin (face, hands, ears)
- Reapply every 2-4 hours in extreme cold
- Prevents moisture loss and wind damage

**Shelf Life:** 6-12 months (cool storage)

**Market Value:** 2-3 sp per tin (2 oz)

**Market Demand:**

## III. SMOKING BEAR MEAT MASTERY

### Why Smoke Bear Meat?

**Smoking transforms bear meat from perishable (3-5 days) to long-lasting (2-12 months) while adding flavor and value.**

**Economic Impact:**
- Fresh ham roast: 2 sp/lb
- Smoked ham roast: 4-5 sp/lb
- **Value increase: +100-150%**

**Preservation Benefits:**
- Fresh meat: 3-5 days shelf life
- Cold-smoked meat: 2-4 months
- Hot-smoked meat (then dried): 6-12 months

---

### Cut Selection for Smoking

**Not all bear cuts smoke equally well. Fat content and size matter.**

**BEST CUTS FOR SMOKING:**

**1. Ham Roasts (Hindquarter)**
- **Why:** Large, fatty, even thickness
- **Weight:** 4-6 lbs each
- **Smoke Time:** 6-8 hours (hot smoke) or 18-24 hours (cold smoke)
- **Result:** Traditional smoked ham (slice for servings)

**2. Shoulder Roasts**
- **Why:** Fatty, tough meat tenderizes well with smoking
- **Weight:** 3-5 lbs each
- **Smoke Time:** 6-8 hours (hot smoke)
- **Result:** Pulled bear meat (shred for sandwiches)

**3. Backstrap (Whole Loin)**
- **Why:** Premium cut, smoking adds luxury value
- **Weight:** 4-6 lbs (whole backstrap)
- **Smoke Time:** 4-6 hours (hot smoke, lower temp)
- **Result:** Sliced smoked loin (premium product)

**4. Ribs (Bone-In Racks)**
- **Why:** Bones add flavor, fat renders during smoking
- **Weight:** 4 lbs per rack
- **Smoke Time:** 5-7 hours (hot smoke)
- **Result:** BBQ-style smoked ribs

**AVOID SMOKING:**
- Ground bear (too small, dries out)
- Stew meat (too small, inconsistent)
- Organs (liver, heart) - smoke poorly

---

### Brine & Cure Formulas

**Curing is CRITICAL before smoking. It prevents spoilage and develops flavor.**

**BASIC DRY CURE (For Hams, Shoulders)**

**Ingredients (per 10 lbs meat):**
- 1/4 cup kosher salt (2.5% of meat weight)
- 2 tbsp brown sugar (2% of meat weight)
- 1 tsp Prague Powder #1 (0.25% - curing salt, prevents botulism)
- 1 tsp black pepper
- 1 tsp garlic powder (optional)

**Process:**
1. Mix all ingredients thoroughly
2. Rub cure mixture ALL OVER meat (coat completely)
3. Place meat in non-reactive container (ceramic, glass)
4. Refrigerate 7-14 days (1 day per inch of thickness)
5. Flip meat every 2-3 days (ensure even curing)
6. After curing: Rinse off excess cure, pat dry
7. Air dry 12-24 hours (forms pellicle - sticky surface for smoke adhesion)
8. NOW ready to smoke

**SWEET CURE (For Backstrap, Premium Cuts)**

**Ingredients (per 10 lbs meat):**
- 1/4 cup kosher salt
- 1/4 cup brown sugar (sweet flavor)
- 2 tbsp maple syrup (optional, extra sweetness)
- 1 tsp Prague Powder #1
- 1 tsp coriander
- 1/2 tsp allspice

**Process:** (same as basic dry cure)

**BRINE CURE (Wet Method - Faster)**

**Ingredients (per gallon of water, treats 10 lbs meat):**
- 1 gallon water
- 3/4 cup kosher salt
- 1/2 cup brown sugar
- 2 tsp Prague Powder #1
- 1 tbsp black peppercorns
- 4 bay leaves
- 1 tbsp juniper berries (optional)

**Process:**
1. Dissolve salt, sugar, Prague Powder in water (heat to dissolve, then cool completely)
2. Add spices
3. Submerge meat in brine (use weight to keep submerged)
4. Refrigerate 5-10 days (1 day per inch of thickness)
5. Remove, rinse, air dry 12-24 hours
6. Ready to smoke

---

### Wood Pairing by Bear Breed

**Different woods create different flavors. Match wood to bear type for optimal results.**

**HICKORY (Strong, Traditional)**
- **Best For:** Black bear, Brown bear (standard flavor)
- **Flavor:** Bacon-like, robust, smoky
- **Smoke Time:** 6-8 hours (hot smoke)
- **Use With:** Ham, shoulder, ribs

**APPLE (Sweet, Mild)**
- **Best For:** Coastal bear (salmon-fed), Cormyrean bear (premium)
- **Flavor:** Sweet, fruity, delicate
- **Smoke Time:** 6-10 hours (cold smoke preferred)
- **Use With:** Backstrap, rib roasts

**OAK (Neutral, Long-Burn)**
- **Best For:** Any bear, especially Polar/Grizzly (large cuts)
- **Flavor:** Neutral, mild, clean smoke
- **Smoke Time:** 8-12 hours (long smoke for large cuts)
- **Use With:** Ham, shoulder, large roasts

**MAPLE (Sweet, Medium)**
- **Best For:** Black bear, Coastal bear
- **Flavor:** Sweet, mellow, slightly caramelized
- **Smoke Time:** 6-8 hours
- **Use With:** Backstrap, ham, ribs

**MESQUITE (Very Strong - Use Sparingly)**
- **Best For:** Grizzly (strong meat can handle strong smoke)
- **Flavor:** Intense, earthy, southwestern
- **Smoke Time:** 3-5 hours (short, strong smoke)
- **Use With:** Ribs, tough cuts

**CHERRY (Sweet, Mild, Red Color)**
- **Best For:** Cormyrean bear (premium presentation)

- **Smoke Time:** 6-8 hours
- **Use With:** Backstrap, premium cuts

**ALDER (Light, Delicate)**
- **Best For:** Coastal bear (salmon-fed, matches seafood tradition)

- **Smoke Time:** 4-6 hours
- **Use With:** Backstrap, tender cuts

### Hot Smoking (Fully Cooked)

**Hot smoking cooks the meat while smoking. Result: Ready to eat, no additional cooking required.**

**Temperature:** 200-250°F

**Time:** 4-8 hours (depending on cut size)

**Process:**

**1. Prepare Smoker:**
- Build fire (charcoal, wood chunks, or dedicated smoker)
- Bring temperature to 225°F (stable)
- Add smoking wood (chunks or chips)

**2. Place Meat:**
- Put cured, dried meat on racks
- Leave space between pieces (air circulation)
- Insert meat thermometer

**3. Smoke:**
- Maintain 200-250°F throughout
- Add wood chunks every 1-2 hours (continuous smoke)
- Monitor internal temperature:
  - Ham/Shoulder: 195-205°F (fully tender)
  - Backstrap: 145-155°F (medium, tender)
  - Ribs: 190-200°F (falling off bone)

**4. Rest:**
- Remove from smoker when temp reached
- Let rest 30-60 minutes (reabsorbs juices)

**5. Storage:**
- Cool completely
- Wrap in wax paper or cloth
- Store in cool, dry place: 2-4 months
- Refrigerate: 4-6 months

**Yield Loss:** 20-30% (moisture evaporation)
- 6 lb fresh ham → 4.5 lb smoked ham

**Value:**
- Fresh: 12 sp (6 lbs × 2 sp/lb)
- Smoked: 22.5 sp (4.5 lbs × 5 sp/lb)
- **Net gain: +10.5 sp per ham (+87% profit increase)**

---

### Cold Smoking (Flavor, Not Cooked)

**Cold smoking adds flavor and preservative smoke WITHOUT cooking. Meat must be cooked later (or dried further).**

**Temperature:** 60-90°F (room temperature, no heat)

**Time:** 12-24 hours (long, slow smoke)

**Process:**

**1. Prepare Smoke Source:**
- Use smoke generator OR offset firebox (smoke travels to chamber, cools before reaching meat)
- Maintain LOW temperature (60-90°F)

**2. Smoke:**
- Hang or rack cured, dried meat
- Apply continuous smoke 12-24 hours
- NO HEAT (if chamber exceeds 100°F, reduce fire or increase distance)

**3. After Smoking:**
- **Option A:** Cook immediately (roast, grill, braise)
- **Option B:** Dry further (hang in cool, dry place 2-4 weeks for dry-cured ham)

**4. Storage:**
- Refrigerate: 2-4 months (then cook before eating)
- Dry-cured: 6-12 months (traditional ham, slice thin, eat raw)

**Best For:**
- Traditional dry-cured hams
- Premium backstrap (smoke, then grill)
- Long-term preservation

## IV. BEAR JERKY PRODUCTION

### Why Make Bear Jerky?

**Jerky is the ultimate preserved meat: lightweight, nutritious, long-lasting.**

**Economics:**
- 10 lbs fresh bear meat: 20 sp (2 sp/lb)
- 3 lbs bear jerky: 18-24 sp (6-8 sp/lb)
- **Value increase: +250-300% per input pound** (despite weight loss)

**Preservation:**
- Fresh meat: 3-5 days
- Jerky: 6-12 months (dry, cool storage)

**Portability:**
- 10 lbs fresh meat = bulky, heavy, spoils fast
- 3 lbs jerky = compact, lightweight, shelf-stable

---

### Lean Cut Selection

**Jerky requires LEAN meat. Fat turns rancid and ruins jerky.**

**BEST CUTS FOR JERKY:**

**1. Ham/Hindquarter (Lean Sections)**
- **Why:** Large, lean muscles, easy to slice
- **Yield:** 12-18 lbs lean meat per 400 lb bear
- **Prep:** Trim ALL visible fat

**2. Shoulder (Lean Sections)**
- **Why:** Affordable, abundant
- **Yield:** 8-12 lbs lean meat
- **Prep:** Remove connective tissue and fat

**3. Neck**
- **Why:** Tough, lean, working muscle
- **Yield:** 4-6 lbs
- **Prep:** Slice thin (tenderizes)

**AVOID:**
- Backstrap (too valuable to make jerky, sell as steaks)
- Ribs (bones, fat)
- Ground bear (wrong texture)
- Belly (too fatty)

---

### Slicing Technique

**Proper slicing is CRITICAL for jerky success.**

**Thickness:** 1/4 inch (consistent)
- Too thick: Won't dry properly, spoilage risk
- Too thin: Over-dries, becomes brittle

**Direction:**
- **With the grain:** Chewier, traditional texture (harder to bite)
- **Against the grain:** Tender, easy to eat (breaks apart easier)
- **Recommendation:** With the grain for adventurers (lasts longer chewing, satisfying), against grain for general market

**Technique:**
1. Partially freeze meat (30-60 min) - firms up for clean slicing
2. Use sharp, long knife
3. Slice consistent 1/4" strips
4. Trim any visible fat

---

### Marinade Recipes

**Marinade adds flavor, salt (preservation), and tenderizes meat.**

**BASIC FRONTIER JERKY MARINADE (Traditional)**

**Ingredients (for 10 lbs lean bear meat):**
- 1 cup soy sauce OR Worcestershire sauce (or substitute: salted fish sauce)
- 1/2 cup water
- 3 tbsp kosher salt
- 2 tbsp black pepper (coarsely ground)
- 2 tbsp brown sugar (tenderizer, balances salt)
- 1 tbsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp onion powder

**Process:**
1. Mix all ingredients in large bowl
2. Add sliced bear meat
3. Toss to coat completely
4. Cover, refrigerate 12-24 hours
5. Stir occasionally (even marination)

---

**SPICED JERKY MARINADE (Exotic)**

**Ingredients (for 10 lbs lean bear meat):**
- 1 cup soy sauce
- 1/2 cup honey (sweetness, tenderizer)
- 1/4 cup apple cider vinegar (tenderizer, tang)
- 3 tbsp kosher salt
- 2 tbsp smoked paprika
- 1 tbsp cumin

### Drying Techniques

**AIR DRYING (Traditional, Free)**

**Process:**
1. Remove meat from marinade, pat dry with cloth
2. Thread meat strips onto string/twine (leave space between pieces)
3. Hang in dry, well-ventilated area:
   - Temperature: 60-75°F
   - Humidity: Low (below 60%)
   - Airflow: Constant (open window, breeze)
4. Dry 24-48 hours
5. Test for doneness (see below)

**Advantages:**
- Free (no equipment)
- Traditional method

**Disadvantages:**
- Weather-dependent (humidity, temperature)
- Slower (24-48 hours)
- Risk of pests (flies, insects) - cover with cheesecloth

---

**SMOKE DRYING (Flavor + Preservation)**

**Process:**
1. Remove meat from marinade, pat dry
2. Place on smoker racks (single layer, no overlap)
3. Smoke at 150-180°F for 6-10 hours
4. Use mild wood (apple, oak)
5. Meat should be dry and leathery

**Advantages:**
- Faster (6-10 hours)
- Adds smoke flavor
- Heat kills bacteria (safer)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires smoker
- Uses wood fuel

---

**DEHYDRATOR (Modern, Consistent)**

**Process:**
1. Remove meat from marinade, pat dry
2. Arrange on dehydrator trays (single layer)
3. Set temperature: 155-165°F
4. Dry 6-12 hours
5. Rotate trays every 2-3 hours (even drying)

**Advantages:**
- Consistent temperature
- Fastest (6-8 hours)
- Safe (controlled heat)

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires dehydrator (expensive)
- No smoke flavor (unless using liquid smoke)

---

### Quality Testing - The Bend Test

**How to know when jerky is done:**

**1. Visual Test:**
- Dark, dry surface
- No moisture beads
- Slightly shrunken

**2. Bend Test (CRITICAL):**
- Pick up a strip
- Bend it slowly
- **Perfect Jerky:** Cracks slightly but doesn't break in half
- **Under-dried:** Bends easily, no crack (needs more time)
- **Over-dried:** Snaps cleanly in half (too brittle, but still safe to eat)

**3. Moisture Test:**
- Cut a piece in half
- Look at interior
- Should be uniform color (no wet center)

### Packaging & Storage

**Proper storage is critical for long shelf life.**

**Packaging:**
- **Best:** Waxed paper or parchment (wrapped tight, then in cloth bag)
- **Good:** Sealed leather pouches
- **Avoid:** Airtight jars (can trap residual moisture, cause mold)

**Storage Conditions:**
- **Cool (40-60°F):** 6-12 months
- **Dry (low humidity):** Essential (moisture = mold)
- **Dark:** Prevents light damage

**Signs of Spoilage:**
- Mold (white, green, black spots) - DISCARD
- Off smell (sour, rancid) - DISCARD
- Slime (moisture, bacteria) - DISCARD

**Market Packaging:**

- 8 oz bundles (week ration)

## V. PELT PRESERVATION & TROPHY PREPARATION

### Field Skinning Technique

**Proper skinning preserves pelt value. Mistakes (cuts, tears) reduce value by 30-50%.**

**Equipment:**
- **Sharp skinning knife** (thin, flexible blade)
- Salt (25-30 lbs for large bear)
- Clean work surface (or hang carcass)

**CASING METHOD (For Trophy Pelts - Grizzly, Polar, Cormyrean)**

**Use when:** Pelt will be mounted (full-body or rug)

**Process:**
1. **Initial Cut:** Cut around feet (just above paws), around head (behind ears)
2. **Ventral Cut:** Cut from throat down belly to tail (CAREFUL: shallow cut, don't puncture hide)
3. **Leg Cuts:** Cut from belly opening down inside of each leg to foot cuts
4. **Peel:** Starting at ventral cut, peel hide away from carcass (use knife to separate connective tissue)
5. **Head:** Carefully cut around eyes, ears, lips (SLOW work, easy to damage)
6. **Remove:** Pelt comes off in one piece (fur-side out)

**Result:** Full pelt with head, paws attached (for full mount)

---

**OPEN METHOD (For Standard Pelts - Black, Brown, Coastal)**

**Use when:** Pelt will be tanned for rugs, cloaks (no full mount)

**Process:**
1. Hang bear by hind legs
2. Cut from anus down belly to throat
3. Cut around legs
4. Peel hide downward (gravity helps)
5. Remove head OR leave head on carcass (if not mounting)

**Result:** Flat pelt (easier to salt/tan)

---

### Fleshing (CRITICAL STEP)

**Fleshing removes all fat, meat, and connective tissue from hide. Skip this = pelt spoils.**

**Process:**
1. Lay pelt flesh-side up on clean surface (or drape over log)
2. Use fleshing knife or draw knife
3. Scrape away ALL fat, meat, membrane (until only clean hide remains)
4. **Be gentle:** Don't cut through hide

**Time:** 1-3 hours (depending on bear size)

**Test:** Run hand over flesh side - should feel smooth, no chunks of fat

---

### Salt Curing (Preservation)

**Salt draws moisture out of hide, prevents bacterial growth (rot).**

**Process:**

**1. Apply Salt:**
- Lay pelt flesh-side up
- Cover ENTIRE flesh side with salt (1/4 inch thick layer)
- Use 15-25 lbs salt for medium bear, 30-40 lbs for grizzly/polar
- **Don't skimp:** Too little salt = pelt spoils

**2. Fold:**
- Fold pelt flesh-to-flesh (salt inside)
- Roll if necessary (for transport)

**3. Drain:**
- Place on sloped surface (liquid will drain out)
- Let sit 48-72 hours
- **Visual:** Salt will turn brown/red from absorbed moisture

**4. Re-salt (Optional but Recommended):**
- Shake off old salt
- Apply fresh salt layer
- Let sit another 24-48 hours

**5. Dry:**
- Hang in shaded, well-ventilated area
- Let air-dry until stiff (3-7 days)
- **Avoid direct sun:** Hardens hide too fast, makes brittle

**Result:** Salt-cured pelt, stable for 6-12 months (can be stored or shipped to tanner)

---

### Brain Tanning (Traditional Method)

**Brain tanning creates soft, supple leather without chemicals.**

**Theory:** Animal brains contain oils and enzymes that break down hide fibers. "Every animal has enough brains to tan its own hide."

**Process (Simplified):**

**1. Rehydrate Salt-Cured Hide:**
- Soak in cool water 12-24 hours
- Change water several times
- Hide should be soft, pliable

**2. Prepare Brain Solution:**
- Cook bear brain in water (simmer 30 minutes)
- Mash brain to paste
- Mix with warm water (1 brain per gallon)

**3. Soak Hide in Brain Solution:**
- Submerge hide completely
- Work brain mixture into hide (massage, knead)
- Soak 2-4 hours

**4. Wring Out:**
- Remove hide, wring out excess liquid
- Stretch hide on frame (or stake to ground)

**5. Soften (Labor-Intensive):**
- While hide dries, repeatedly stretch, pull, work it
- Keep working until completely dry and soft
- **Critical:** If you stop working it, hide will dry stiff

**6. Smoke (Optional - Waterproofs):**
- Hang hide over smoke pit (cool smoke, no heat)
- Smoke 4-8 hours
- Smoke creates water-resistant barrier

**Result:** Soft, supple, buckskin-like leather

**Time:** 2-3 days active work

**Advantages:**
- Traditional, no chemicals
- Softest leather
- Cultural authenticity

**Disadvantages:**
- Labor-intensive (constant stretching/working)
- Requires skill (easy to mess up)
- Not water-resistant (unless smoked)

---

### Chemical Tanning (Modern/Professional Method)

**Chemical tanning uses alum, tannins, or chromium salts to preserve hide.**

**ALUM TANNING (Safest for Home Use):**

**Ingredients:**
- 1 lb alum (potassium aluminum sulfate)
- 1 lb non-iodized salt
- 4 gallons water

**Process:**
1. Rehydrate salt-cured hide (12-24 hour soak)
2. Dissolve alum and salt in warm water
3. Submerge hide completely
4. Soak 7-10 days (stir daily)
5. Remove, rinse, wring out
6. Stretch and work hide until dry
7. Oil with neatsfoot oil or bear grease (softens)

**Result:** Preserved, water-resistant leather

**Time:** 10-14 days (mostly passive soaking)

**Advantages:**
- Easier than brain tanning
- More consistent results
- Water-resistant

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires chemicals (alum)
- Not as soft as brain tan

---

**PROFESSIONAL TANNING (Best for Trophy Pelts):**

**Process:**
1. Salt-cure pelt (as described above)
2. Ship to professional tanner
3. Tanner uses industrial chemicals (chromium, tannins)
4. Returns finished pelt

**Cost:** 10-20 sp (depending on size, quality requested)

**Result:** Professional-grade pelt (soft, durable, water-resistant)

**Best For:**
- Trophy mounts (grizzly, polar, Cormyrean)
- High-value pelts (100+ gp)
- When labor time is worth more than tanning cost

### Trophy Mount Preparation

**Full bear mount requires professional taxidermist, but you can prepare materials.**

**What Taxidermist Needs:**

## VI. ALCHEMICAL HARVESTING

### Gallbladder Identification & Removal

**The gallbladder is a small organ attached to the liver. It stores bile (digestive fluid) which has medicinal/alchemical value.**

**Location:**
- Under ribcage, right side
- Attached to underside of liver
- **Visual:** Small, green/brown sac (2-4 oz, pear-shaped)

**CRITICAL: Do NOT puncture gallbladder during removal. Bile release ruins it.**

---

**STEP-BY-STEP REMOVAL:**

**1. Expose Liver:**
- Open body cavity
- Identify liver (large, reddish-brown organ)

**2. Locate Gallbladder:**
- Look at underside of liver
- Find small sac connected by duct

**3. Isolate:**
- Carefully cut connective tissue around gallbladder
- **Leave duct attached** (for now)

**4. Clamp Duct:**
- Tie off bile duct with string (prevents bile leaking)
- Tie BOTH sides (above and below gallbladder)

**5. Remove:**
- Cut duct between ties
- Remove gallbladder carefully
- **Check for leaks:** If punctured, value drops 50-80%

**6. Clean:**
- Gently rinse exterior with cool water
- Pat dry

---

### Bile Extraction & Bottling (Liquid Form)

**Some alchemists prefer liquid bile (easier to measure for potions).**

**Process:**

**1. Puncture Gallbladder:**
- Hold over glass vial or bottle
- Make small cut in gallbladder wall
- Squeeze gently to express bile

**2. Collect:**
- Bile is thick, greenish-brown liquid
- Typical yield: 1-3 fl oz per bear

**3. Bottle:**
- Use small glass vials (ceramic or glass only, bile reacts with metal)
- Seal tightly with cork + wax

**4. Label:**
- Bear species (affects properties)
- Date harvested
- Weight/volume

**Storage:**
- Cool, dark location
- Shelf life: 12-18 months (liquid)

**Value:**
- Same as whole gallbladder (bile extracted = same value)
- Some alchemists pay slight premium (+10-15%) for pre-extracted bile

---

### Drying Whole Gallbladders (Traditional Method)

**Dried gallbladders store longer and ship easier.**

**Process:**

**1. Clean:**
- Rinse exterior gently
- Remove any attached tissue

**2. Dry:**
- Hang in cool, dry, dark location
- Air circulates around it
- Takes 2-4 weeks to fully dry

**3. Test for Dryness:**
- Should be hard, brittle
- No soft spots (indicates incomplete drying)

**4. Store:**
- Wrap in paper or cloth
- Store in dry container
- **Keep dry:** Moisture rehydrates it (spoilage)

**Storage:**
- Cool, dry, dark
- Shelf life: 2-3 years (dried)

**Rehydration (for alchemist use):**
- Soak dried gallbladder in water 12-24 hours
- Bile reconstitutes
- Use as liquid bile

---
