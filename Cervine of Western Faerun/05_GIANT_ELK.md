### **RUT SEASON AGGRESSION (MARPENOTH - UKTAR)**

**BEHAVIORAL CHANGES:**

**Bulls During Rut:**
- Aggression increases 300-500%
- Will charge ANYTHING perceived as threat or rival (humanoids, horses, wagons)
- Charge distance: 100-200 yards (will charge from this range on sight)
- Territoriality: Defend 1-3 square mile territories against all intruders
- Vocalizations: Deep roaring/bellowing, can be heard 1-2 miles
- Activity: 80-90% of daylight hours active (vs 40-50% rest of year)

**Physical Changes:**
- Bulls lose 200-400 lbs body weight (use fat reserves, minimal eating)
- Neck swells 30-50% (muscle development for fighting)
- Dewlap swells/darkens
- Urine-soaking behavior (roll in urine wallows, pungent odor detectable 200+ yards)
- Eyes bloodshot, aggressive posture

**DANGER INDICATORS (IMMEDIATE THREAT):**

If a Giant Elk bull displays ANY of the following, **RETREAT IMMEDIATELY:**

1. **Ears pinned back** (flat against neck)
2. **Hackles raised** (shoulder hair standing up)
3. **Head lowered** (antlers pointing forward)
4. **Pawing ground** (front hooves)
5. **Grunting/huffing** (short, explosive vocalizations)
6. **Direct stare** (fixed eye contact)
7. **Closing distance** (walking toward you, even slowly)

**If elk breaks into RUN toward you = CHARGE IN PROGRESS**
- **DO NOT RUN** (elk is faster - 60 ft/round vs human 30 ft/round)
- **GET BEHIND LARGE TREE** (>18 inch diameter)
- **CLIMB if possible** (elk cannot climb, will not jump >4 ft vertically)
- **HOLD POSITION behind cover** (charge may be bluff, may break off)
- **IF KNOCKED DOWN:** Cover head/neck, curl into ball, play dead

**RUT SEASON HUNTING RECOMMENDATION:**
### **DO NOT HUNT GIANT ELK DURING RUT (Marpenoth-Uktar).**

**Reasons:**
1. Bulls are hyper-aggressive (charge on sight, 100+ yard range)

3. Fatality rate triples (36-45% vs 12-20% normal)

**If you MUST hunt during rut (trophy antlers at peak):**
- Increase party size to 6-8 adventurers

## III. PROCESSING SPECIFICATIONS

### **FIELD DRESSING (IMMEDIATE POST-HARVEST)**

**‚ö†Ô∏è FIELD DRESSING A GIANT ELK REQUIRES MINIMUM 4-6 PEOPLE ‚ö†Ô∏è**

**Unlike smaller cervines (deer, wapiti, even moose) that CAN be field dressed solo:**
- **Giant Elk CANNOT be field dressed by 1-2 people**
- Carcass weight (2,000 lbs) requires mechanical advantage for positioning
- Gutting requires multiple people to hold/manipulate body cavity
- **Attempting solo field dressing = high risk of injury (crush injuries, hernias)**

**REQUIRED EQUIPMENT:**

**Cutting Tools:**
- [ ] Large fixed-blade knives (8-10 inch, multiple)
- [ ] Bone saw (minimum 18 inch blade)
- [ ] Hatchet or camp axe (for pelvic/sternum splitting)
- [ ] Sharpening stone (knives dull quickly on hide)
- [ ] Backup knife (primary WILL dull/break)

**Rigging/Movement:**
- [ ] Block and tackle OR portable winch (critical)
- [ ] 200+ feet of heavy rope (3/4 inch minimum diameter)
- [ ] Sturdy tree limb or A-frame setup (for hoisting/positioning)
- [ ] 6-10 pack animals (mules or draft horses)
- [ ] Tarps (for laying out carcass, protecting meat)

**Preservation:**
- [ ] Cheesecloth or game bags (for quarters, protect from flies)
- [ ] Salt (50-80 lbs for initial preservation)
- [ ] Ice (if available, for rapid cooling)
- [ ] Pepper (deterrent for insects)

**FIELD DRESSING PROCEDURE:**

**Step 1: Initial Positioning (30-60 minutes, 4-6 people)**

1. **Locate suitable processing site:**
   - Flat ground (or slight incline)
   - Sturdy tree with 12-15 ft high limb (for hoist rigging)
   - OR bring A-frame structure (portable)
   - Access for pack animals
   - Water source nearby (for cleaning)

2. **Position carcass on back:**
   - Use ropes + multiple people to roll onto back
   - Prop with logs/rocks to prevent rolling
   - Position head uphill if on slope

3. **Rig hoist system:**
   - Run rope over tree limb or through block and tackle
   - Attach to hind legs (above hocks)
   - **DO NOT attempt to hoist entire carcass** (too heavy, rope/limb will break)
   - Hoist raises hind end 2-3 ft (provides angle for drainage, easier gutting)

**Step 2: Opening the Body Cavity (45-90 minutes)**

4. **Remove genitals/udder:**
   - Bulls: Cut around scrotum/penis, remove carefully (avoid puncturing bladder)
   - Cows: Cut around udder, remove
   - **DO NOT puncture bladder or intestines** (ruins meat)

5. **Split pelvis:**
   - Use bone saw or hatchet
   - Cut along centerline of pelvis (separates hip bones)
   - This opens access to lower GI tract
   - **Requires 2 people:** 1 cutting, 1 holding legs apart

6. **Open body cavity (sternum to pelvis):**
   - Start at sternum (breastbone)
   - Insert knife blade-up (cutting from inside prevents puncturing stomach/intestines)
   - Cut along centerline, belly to pelvis
   - **Requires 2 people:** 1 cutting, 1 holding hide back
   - **GO SLOW** - puncturing stomach/intestines = meat contamination

7. **Split sternum (optional but recommended):**
   - Use bone saw or hatchet
   - Split breastbone along centerline
   - Opens chest cavity, easier access to heart/lungs
   - **Requires significant force** (sternum is thick bone)

**Step 3: Removing Organs (60-90 minutes)**

8. **Remove intestines/stomach:**
   - Cut connective tissue attaching organs to body wall
   - Work from pelvis forward
   - **Requires 2-3 people:** 1-2 cutting, 1 pulling/holding organs
   - Pull organs out CAREFULLY (avoid rupturing)
   - **Total gut weight:** 180-250 lbs (requires multiple people to remove)

9. **Remove heart/lungs/liver:**
   - Cut diaphragm (muscle separating chest/abdomen)
   - Reach into chest cavity
   - Cut connective tissue attaching heart/lungs to rib cage
   - Remove heart (4-6 lbs, SAVE - valuable)
   - Remove liver (12-18 lbs, SAVE if undamaged)
   - Remove lungs (discard unless damaged by shot = inspect for viability)

10. **Remove bladder/remaining organs:**
    - Carefully cut bladder free (DO NOT puncture)
    - Remove kidneys (SAVE, 2-3 lbs total)
    - Remove remaining connective tissue

**Step 4: Initial Cleaning and Cooling (30-60 minutes)**

11. **Wipe out body cavity:**
    - Use clean cloths or dry grasses
    - Remove blood, debris, bone fragments (from saw cuts)
    - **DO NOT use water** (promotes bacterial growth if cannot dry quickly)

12. **Prop chest cavity open:**
    - Insert sticks or cleaned rib spreader
    - Allows airflow, promotes cooling
    - **Critical in warm weather** (above 50¬∞F)

13. **Cover carcass:**
    - Drape with cheesecloth or game bags
    - Protects from flies, debris
    - **DO NOT use solid tarps** (traps heat, promotes spoilage)

**YIELD FROM FIELD DRESSING:**

**Starting Weight:** 2,000 lb bull (average)

**Removed in Field Dressing:**
- Hide (if removed in field): 180-220 lbs (9-11% of live weight)
- Head (if removed): 80-110 lbs (4-5.5%)
- Lower legs/hooves: 60-80 lbs (3-4%)
- Organs/intestines: 180-250 lbs (9-12.5%)
- Blood: 80-100 lbs (4-5%)
- **TOTAL REMOVED:** 580-760 lbs (29-38% of live weight)

**DRESSED CARCASS WEIGHT:** 1,240-1,420 lbs (62-71% of live weight)

**For processing calculations, use 65% dressing yield:**
- 2,000 lb bull √ó 65% = **1,300 lbs dressed weight**

### **QUARTERING AND TRANSPORT (WILDERNESS TO BUTCHER SHOP)**

**‚ö†Ô∏è GIANT ELK CANNOT BE TRANSPORTED WHOLE ‚ö†Ô∏è**

**Unlike smaller cervines:**
- Whitetail: Can hang whole carcass (117 lbs dressed)
- Wapiti: Can hang whole carcass with strong beam (490 lbs dressed)
- Moose: Typically quartered (780 lbs dressed, too heavy for most beams)
- **Giant Elk: MUST be quartered in field** (1,300 lbs dressed, no practical way to transport whole)

**QUARTERING PROCEDURE:**

**Step 1: Remove Front Quarters (60-90 minutes, 2-3 people per quarter)**

1. **Locate shoulder blade (scapula):**
   - Run hand along inside of front leg
   - Feel for shoulder blade attached to rib cage by muscle/connective tissue (no bone joint)

2. **Cut along shoulder blade:**
   - Insert knife between shoulder blade and rib cage
   - Cut connective tissue (subscapularis muscle)
   - Pull leg away from body while cutting
   - **Front quarter separates completely** (no need to saw bone)

3. **Repeat for second front quarter**

**Front quarter weight (each):** 160-200 lbs √ó 2 = **320-400 lbs total**

**Step 2: Remove Hindquarters (90-120 minutes, 3-4 people per quarter)**

4. **Separate hindquarter from spine:**

   - Cut through hip joint OR
   - Saw through pelvis/spine junction
   - **Requires bone saw + significant effort**

5. **Remove entire hindquarter:**
   - Cut remaining muscle/connective tissue
   - Separate leg from carcass
   - **Requires 3-4 people to lower/control** (200-250 lbs each)

6. **Repeat for second hindquarter**

**Hindquarter weight (each):** 200-250 lbs √ó 2 = **400-500 lbs total**

**Step 3: Remove Backstrap/Tenderloins (30-45 minutes)**

7. **Remove backstraps (outside of spine):**
   - Run knife along spine (outside of rib cage)
   - Follow natural seam between loin and rib muscles
   - Remove entire backstrap (each side of spine)
   - **Most valuable cut by weight**

8. **Remove tenderloins (inside of body cavity):**
   - Reach inside body cavity
   - Feel for tenderloins against inside of spine
   - Cut free from spine
   - Remove (usually comes out in one piece per side)

**Backstrap weight:** 35-45 lbs total (both sides)
**Tenderloin weight:** 12-16 lbs total (both sides)

9. **Remaining carcass (rib cage, neck, spine):**

**Why longer aging?**
- Muscle fibers thicker (older, more developed animals)

- Humidity: 70-75%
- Air circulation: Moderate (prevent mold, promote drying)
- Trim external "bark" (dried surface) before cutting

### **ANTLER GROWTH CYCLE**

**Annual cycle:**

**LATE WINTER (Late Alturiak - Early Ches):**
- Bulls shed antlers (drop from skull plates)
- Shedding triggers: Dropping testosterone after rut, increasing daylight
- Both antlers typically drop within 24-72 hours of each other
- **Shed antler value:** 80-180 gp each (160-360 gp matched pair)

**SPRING (Ches - Mirtul):**
- New antler growth begins
- Antlers covered in "velvet" (soft, blood-rich skin)
- Growth rate: 1-2 inches per week (fastest-growing tissue in animal kingdom)
- Bulls are GENTLE during velvet (avoid damaging growing antlers)

**SUMMER (Kythorn - Eleasis):**
- Continued velvet growth
- Antlers reach full size by late Eleasis
- Bulls increase calcium/phosphorus intake (chew bones, antlers, mineral licks)

**EARLY FALL (Eleint):**
- Velvet hardens, blood supply cuts off
- Bulls rub antlers on trees (remove velvet, polish bone)
- Antlers fully hardened by mid-Eleint (ready for rut)
- **Velvet harvest window:** Early Eleint (3-5 day window)
  - Fresh-shed velvet: 40-120 gp (alchemical, virility tonics)
  - **RARE:** Bulls are aggressive/defensive during velvet shedding

**FALL - RUT (Marpenoth - Uktar):**
- Antlers at PEAK condition (polished, hard, sharp)
- Bulls use antlers for:
  - Territorial displays (thrashing trees, brush)
  - Combat with rival bulls (sparring, shoving matches, goring)
  - Intimidation (visual display to cows, rivals)

**WINTER (Nightal - Alturiak):**
- Post-rut deterioration
- Antlers may chip, crack, break (from fighting)
- Testosterone drops
- Antlers prepare to shed (bone weakens at skull plate)

### **FIELD SAFETY DURING HUNT**

**TRACKING AND APPROACH:**

**1. SIGNS OF GIANT ELK:**
- Tracks: 6-8 inches long, deep impressions (weight), cloven hoof
- Scat: Large pellets (2-3 inches), cylindrical
- Rubs: Trees with bark stripped (bulls rub antlers), 6-9 ft high
- Wallows: Muddy depressions (bulls roll in during rut), pungent odor
- Trails: Wide paths through brush (broken branches 7-9 ft high)

**2. WIND DIRECTION (CRITICAL):**
- Giant Elk have excellent sense of smell (detect humans 200+ yards downwind)
- **ALWAYS approach from downwind** (wind blowing from elk toward you)
- If elk catches your scent = WILL flee or charge (rut bulls)

**3. STALKING DISTANCE:**
- **Ideal shot range:** 80-120 yards (outside charge range, within accurate shot)
- **Minimum safe distance:** 150+ yards (rut bulls may charge from this range)
- **DO NOT close to <80 yards** (charge is almost guaranteed if detected)

**SHOT PLACEMENT:**

**Target vital zones:**

**1. Heart/Lung (BEST):**
- Location: Behind front shoulder, 1/3 up from belly line
- Shot: Broadside or slight quartering angle
- Result: Rapid blood loss, elk down within 50-200 yards
- **SUCCESS RATE:** 70-85% one-shot kill

**2. Neck (HIGH RISK/HIGH REWARD):**
- Location: Mid-neck, spine or major arteries
- Shot: Requires precision, small target
- Result: Instant drop (spine) OR rapid bleed (arteries)
- **SUCCESS RATE:** 40-60% (high miss rate, wounding common)

**3. Head/Brain (NOT RECOMMENDED):**
- Location: Small target, thick skull
- Shot: Extremely difficult, moving target
- Result: Instant kill IF successful, BUT high miss/wounding rate
- **SUCCESS RATE:** 15-30% (experts only)

**AVOID:**
- Gut shots (elk suffers, spoils meat, long death)
- Rear/ham shots (poor lethality, wounding)
- Shoulder bone shots (deflects arrow/bolt, wounding)

**MULTIPLE SHOOTERS:**
- **Coordinate shots:** Count "1, 2, 3, SHOOT" (all fire simultaneously)
- Multiple hits = faster kill (less suffering, less danger)
- Reduces risk of wounding without killing

---

### **EMERGENCY RESPONSE PROTOCOLS**

**IF ELK CHARGES:**

**1. IMMEDIATE ACTIONS:**
- **SHOUT WARNING:** "CHARGE!" (alert party)
- **DO NOT RUN** (elk is faster, 60 ft/round vs human 30 ft)
- **GET BEHIND COVER:** Large tree (18+ inch diameter), boulder, terrain feature
- **READY MELEE WEAPON:** Spear/pike (brace against charge)

**2. PARTY RESPONSE:**
- **Ranged attackers:** FOCUS FIRE on charging elk (all shots on charger)
- **Tank/frontline:** Intercept if possible (block charge, take hit)
- **Healer:** Prepare healing (assume someone will be hit)

**3. IF HIT BY CHARGE:**
- Charge attack: +6 to hit, 2d6+4 damage (AVG 11) + DC 14 STR save or knocked prone
- **IF KNOCKED PRONE:** Elk follows with hooves attack: +6 to hit, 4d8+4 damage (AVG 22)
- **TOTAL DAMAGE:** Up to 33 damage in one round (instant death for level 1-2, critical injury for level 3-4)

**4. AFTER CHARGE:**
- **IF INJURED:** Healer IMMEDIATELY stabilizes/heals
- **IF ELK CONTINUES AGGRESSION:** RETREAT (do not re-engage)
- **IF ELK FLEES:** DO NOT PURSUE (risk of ambush/second charge)

---

**IF PARTY MEMBER DOWNED:**

**1. IMMEDIATE ACTIONS:**
- **Remaining party:** Focus fire on elk (kill or drive off)
- **DO NOT approach downed member until elk neutralized** (risk of trampling)

**2. STABILIZATION (once elk neutralized):**
- Check breathing, pulse
- Cast Cure Wounds or use healing potion
- **IF NO HEALING AVAILABLE:** Stabilize with Medicine check (DC 10)

**3. INJURY ASSESSMENT:**
- Broken bones: Splint, immobilize, prepare travois (cannot walk)
- Internal bleeding: Requires Lesser Restoration or extended rest
- Unconscious: Monitor, keep warm, heal as soon as possible

**4. RETREAT DECISION:**
- **IF serious injury (>50% HP loss, broken bones, unconscious):** ABORT HUNT, return to civilization
- **IF minor injury (<30% HP loss):** Continue BUT increase caution

---

**IF LOST/SEPARATED:**

**1. STOP MOVING:**
- Do NOT wander (makes finding you harder)
- Stay in one location

**2. SIGNAL:**
- Horn blast (3 short, 3 long, 3 short = distress)
- Smoke signal (build fire, add green wood for smoke)
- Light spell (if available, visible at night)

**3. WAIT:**
- Party will search for you (return to last known location)
- If no contact within 4-6 hours, prepare shelter

**4. SURVIVAL:**
- Build shelter (lean-to, snow cave if winter)
- Fire (warmth, signaling)
- Water (find stream, purify)
- Food (ration supplies, forage if able)

### **POST-HARVEST SAFETY**

**FIELD DRESSING HAZARDS:**

**1. KNIFE INJURIES:**
- Slips while gutting (puncture wounds, cuts)
- **Prevention:** Cut AWAY from body, use sharp knives (dull = requires force = slips)

**2. PHYSICAL STRAIN:**
- Lifting heavy quarters (200-250 lbs) = back injury, hernias
- **Prevention:** Use 3-4 people per quarter, mechanical advantage (ropes, pulleys)

**3. BLOOD-BORNE DISEASES:**
- Rare but possible (chronic wasting disease - NOT in Giant Elk, but general caution)
- **Prevention:** Wear gloves, avoid contact with brain/spinal tissue, wash hands thoroughly

**TRANSPORT HAZARDS:**

**1. PACK ANIMAL ACCIDENTS:**
- Mules spooked by predators (wolves, bears)
- Lost footing on mountain trails (pack falls, injury)
- **Prevention:** Secure loads, experienced handler, scout trails ahead

**2. MEAT SPOILAGE:**
- Warm weather (>55¬∞F) = rapid spoilage (2-4 days)
- **Prevention:** Salt meat immediately, move fast, smoke/dry if delayed

**3. PREDATOR ATTRACTION:**
- Blood scent attracts wolves, bears (attack pack animals or camp)
- **Prevention:** Hang meat 10+ ft high at night, post watches, fires
