## PURPOSE OF THIS CHAPTER

This chapter establishes the **baseline processing standards** that apply to ALL 12 deer breeds in this guide. Every breed-specific chapter references these universal standards and notes only their specific variations.

**What's Universal:**
- The 7 cervine primals (same for all deer, scaled by size)
- Yield percentages (65% dressing, 45% retail - consistent across breeds)
- Processing procedures (field dressing, aging, fabrication)
- Lean meat handling (2-3% body fat - no fat rendering economics)
- Butcher's choice options (steaks vs roasts)

**What Varies by Breed:**
- Live weight (40 lbs roe deer to 2,200 lbs giant elk)
- Antler size/value (small roe spikes to massive giant elk racks)
- Meat flavor (diet-dependent: grass-fed vs lichen vs oak mast)
- Hide thickness (roe deer thin vs moose armor-grade)
- Processing time (4 hours roe deer to 24 hours giant elk)

## I. DEER ANATOMY OVERVIEW

### Physical Structure

**Cervine Body Plan:**
- **Ruminant herbivore:** Four-chamber stomach, cud-chewing
- **Lean build:** 2-3% body fat (vs bear 8-10%, pork 15-25%, cattle 5-15%)
- **Long legs:** Optimized for running/jumping (vs bear's stocky power build)
- **Antlers (males only, most species):** Renewable bone tissue, annual growth/shedding
- **Thin hide:** Lighter than bear/cattle (easier to process, less protection)

**Key Anatomical Differences from Bear:**
- **No significant fat layer:** Minimal subcutaneous fat (no rendering economics)
- **Smaller organs:** Liver, heart proportionally smaller than bear
- **Longer legs/shanks:** More primal cuts (4 shanks vs bear 0)
- **Thin hide:** Faster skinning, less tanning labor

**Key Anatomical Differences from Cattle:**
- **Wild vs domestic:** Lean muscle, no marbling (vs cattle grain-fed marbling)
- **Antlers vs horns:** Deer shed/regrow annually, cattle keep permanent horns
- **Smaller organs:** Less organ meat value than cattle
- **Faster growth:** Deer mature 1-2 years, cattle 2-4 years (affects meat texture)

## II. UNIVERSAL YIELD FORMULA

### The Cervine Standard: 65% Dressing, 45% Retail

**BASELINE EXAMPLE: 180 lb Sword Coast Whitetail (Adult Male)**

**Step 1: Live Weight to Dressed Weight (65%)**
- **Live Weight:** 180 lbs (whole deer, field condition)
- **Dressing %:** 65% (blood, hide, head, hooves, organs removed)
- **Dressed Weight:** 180 lbs × 65% = **117 lbs** (carcass only)

**What's Removed (35% loss):**
- Blood: 8 lbs (4-5% of live weight, immediate drainage)
- Hide: 12 lbs (6-7%, with head attached)
- Head: 10 lbs (5-6%, skull + brain)
- Hooves: 4 lbs (2%, all four legs)
- Organs (heart, liver, lungs, intestines): 22 lbs (12%)
- Trimmed fat/connective tissue: 7 lbs (4%)
- **Total Loss:** 63 lbs (35% of live weight)

**Step 2: Dressed Weight to Retail Cuts (45%)**
- **Dressed Weight:** 117 lbs (hanging carcass)
- **Retail %:** 45% of dressed (bone, trim, additional waste removed)
- **Retail Weight:** 117 lbs × 45% = **52-53 lbs** (final sellable cuts)

**What's Removed (55% of dressed = additional loss):**
- Bone (vertebrae, ribs, leg bones): 35 lbs (30% of dressed)
- Trim/sinew/connective tissue: 18 lbs (15%)
- Additional fat trim: 4 lbs (3%)
- Shrinkage during aging (moisture loss): 8 lbs (7%)
- **Total Loss from Dressed:** 65 lbs (55% of dressed carcass)

**FINAL TOTAL YIELD:**
- **180 lbs live → 52 lbs retail = 29% overall yield**

**This is UNIVERSAL across all deer breeds** (small variations ±1-2% depending on condition/age)

---

### Yield Comparison (Deer vs Other Animals)

| Animal | Live Weight | Dressing % | Retail % (of dressed) | Total Live→Retail % |
|--------|-------------|------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Deer (Cervine)** | Variable | 65% | 45% | **29%** (lean, wild) |
| **Bear (Ursine)** | Variable | 75% | 49% | **37%** (fatty, wild) |
| **Cattle (Bovine)** | Variable | 60% | 65% | **39%** (domestic, marbled) |
| **Pig (Porcine)** | Variable | 70% | 60% | **42%** (domestic, fatty) |

**Why Deer Have Lower Yield:**
- **Lean meat:** No fat layer to add weight (bear has 8-10% fat = +8-10 lbs on 100 lb animal)
- **Wild game:** More bone, sinew, connective tissue than domestic animals
- **Long legs:** Proportionally more bone in shanks vs stocky animals
- **Thin hide:** Lighter hide means less % credited to carcass weight

## III. THE 7 CERVINE PRIMALS

### Primal Overview

**The 7 Primals (Front to Back, Top to Bottom):**
1. **NECK** - Tough, working muscle (grinding, stew)
2. **SHOULDER** - Moderately tough, flavorful (roasts, grinding)
3. **RIB** - Premium, tender (chops, rib roasts)
4. **LOIN (Backstrap)** - PREMIUM, most tender (steaks, whole roasts)
5. **SIRLOIN** - Tender, transitional (steaks, roasts)
6. **HAM (Hindquarter)** - Large, moderately tender (steaks, roasts, curing)
7. **SHANK** - Tough, collagen-rich (braising, osso buco, soup bones)

**Note:** Unlike bear (8 primals including belly fat), deer have 7 primals due to minimal fat layer.

---

### PRIMAL 1: NECK

**Location:** Cervical vertebrae region, base of skull to shoulder
**Weight:** 6-8% of dressed weight (7-9 lbs on 117 lb carcass)
**Character:** Tough, working muscle (constant movement), lots of connective tissue
**Fat Content:** Minimal (1-2%)

**Processing:**
- **Remove whole:** Cut behind skull, separate at C7 vertebrae (shoulder junction)
- **Bone-in or boneless:** Can leave vertebrae for soup bones OR debone for ground meat

**Best Uses:**
- **Ground venison:** Primary use (tough meat, excellent flavor when ground)
- **Stew meat:** Cut into 1-inch cubes (requires long, slow cooking)
- **Soup bones:** If left bone-in (marrow, stock, collagen)

**Retail Cuts:**
- Ground venison: 6-7 lbs
- Soup bones (if bone-in): 8-9 lbs total weight

**Pricing:** 1-1.5 sp/lb (utility grade, not premium)

---

### PRIMAL 2: SHOULDER (Front Quarter)

**Location:** Scapula (shoulder blade) region, front leg attachment
**Weight:** 18-20% of dressed weight (21-23 lbs on 117 lb carcass)
**Character:** Moderately tough, flavorful, well-worked muscle
**Fat Content:** Minimal (2-3%)

**Processing:**
- **Separate from rib:** Cut between 5th-6th rib
- **Remove from carcass:** Follow natural seam along scapula
- **Debone:** Remove shoulder blade (complex bone structure, leave for butcher)

**Best Uses:**
- **Shoulder roasts:** 3-4 lb roasts (slow-cook, pot roast style)
- **Grinding:** Excellent flavor for venison burger/sausage
- **Braising:** Whole or cubed (low/slow with liquid)

**Butcher's Choice:**

**Option A: Shoulder Roasts**
- 3× Shoulder Roast (3-4 lbs each) @ 1.5-2 sp/lb = 4.5-8 sp each
- Total: 13.5-24 sp
- Use: Family meals, slow-cooker, pot roast

**Option B: Ground Venison (from shoulder)**
- 10-11 lbs ground @ 1.5 sp/lb = 1.5 sp each
- Total: 15-16.5 sp
- Use: Burgers, sausage, meatballs, versatile

**Retail Pricing:** Roasts = 1.5-2 sp/lb, Ground = 1.5 sp/lb

---

### PRIMAL 3: RIB

**Location:** Rib cage, ribs 6-12 (thoracic region)
**Weight:** 8-10% of dressed weight (9-12 lbs on 117 lb carcass)
**Character:** Tender, premium (less-worked muscle), good flavor
**Fat Content:** Minimal (2-3%, mostly intermuscular)

**Processing:**
- **Separate from loin:** Cut along spine, parallel to backbone
- **Remove from carcass:** Follow rib bones

**Best Uses:**
- **Rib chops:** Individual bone-in steaks (1-1.5 inch thick)
- **Rib roast:** Whole rack (bone-in, elegant presentation)
- **Frenched racks:** Clean rib bones for presentation (high-end preparation)

**Butcher's Choice:**

**Option A: Rib Roast (Whole Rack)**
- 2× Rib Roast (4-5 lbs each) @ 3-4 sp/lb = 12-20 sp each
- Total: 24-40 sp
- Use: Special occasion, oven roasting, premium

**Option B: Rib Chops**
- 7-9× Rib Chop (12-14 oz each) @ 2.5-3.5 sp each = 2.5-3.5 sp each
- Total: 17.5-31.5 sp
- Use: Individual servings, grilling, quick-cook

**Retail Pricing:** Rib roast = 3-4 sp/lb, Rib chops = 2.5-3.5 sp each

---

### PRIMAL 4: LOIN (Backstrap) - **PREMIUM CUT**

**Location:** Along spine, entire back length (thoracic to lumbar vertebrae)
**Weight:** 6-7% of dressed weight (7-8 lbs on 117 lb carcass)
**Character:** **MOST TENDER**, premium, never worked muscle
**Fat Content:** Minimal (1-2%, almost zero)

**Processing:**
- **Remove from carcass:** Cut along spine on both sides, separate from vertebrae
- **Results in:** 2 whole backstraps (3.5-4 lbs each)

**Best Uses:**
- **Backstrap steaks:** Premium individual steaks (1-1.5 inch thick)
- **Whole backstrap roasts:** Special occasion, oven roasting (rare to medium-rare ONLY)
- **Medallions:** Thin slices (6-8 oz), quick sear

**Butcher's Choice:**

**Option A: Whole Backstraps**
- 2× Whole Backstrap (3.5-4 lbs each) @ 5-7 sp/lb = 17.5-28 sp each
- Total: 35-56 sp
- Use: Special orders, whole roasting, premium buyers

**Option B: Backstrap Steaks (RECOMMENDED FOR RETAIL)**
- 10-12× Backstrap Steak (10-12 oz each) @ 4-6 sp each = 4-6 sp each
- Total: 40-72 sp
- Use: Individual portions, quick-cook, highest-value option

**Retail Pricing:** Whole = 5-7 sp/lb, Steaks = 4-6 sp each

**CRITICAL COOKING NOTE:** Backstrap is LEAN. Overcooking = dry, tough, ruined. Cook to medium-rare (135-145°F) ONLY.

---

### PRIMAL 5: SIRLOIN

**Location:** Between loin and hindquarter, hip region
**Weight:** 8-9% of dressed weight (9-10 lbs on 117 lb carcass)
**Character:** Tender, transitional between loin and ham
**Fat Content:** Minimal (2%)

**Processing:**
- **Separate from loin:** Cut at last lumbar vertebrae
- **Separate from ham:** Cut at hip joint

**Best Uses:**
- **Sirloin steaks:** Individual steaks (10-12 oz)
- **Sirloin roast:** Whole (2-3 lbs), oven roasting
- **Tip roast:** If separated into sub-primals

**Butcher's Choice:**

**Option A: Sirloin Roast**
- 2× Sirloin Roast (4-5 lbs each) @ 3-4 sp/lb = 12-20 sp each
- Total: 24-40 sp

**Option B: Sirloin Steaks**
- 6-8× Sirloin Steak (12-14 oz each) @ 3-4 sp each = 3-4 sp each
- Total: 18-32 sp

**Retail Pricing:** 3-4 sp/lb (slightly less than backstrap, more than ham)

---

### PRIMAL 6: HAM (Hindquarter)

**Location:** Rear leg, femur region
**Weight:** 20-22% of dressed weight (23-26 lbs on 117 lb carcass, LARGEST PRIMAL)
**Character:** Moderately tender, large muscle groups, excellent for steaks/roasts
**Fat Content:** Minimal (2-3%)

**Processing:**
- **Separate from sirloin:** Cut at hip joint
- **Debone OR leave bone-in:** Can process as bone-in ham roasts OR debone for steaks

**Best Uses:**
- **Ham steaks:** Individual steaks from rounds (10-14 oz)
- **Ham roasts:** Large roasts (4-6 lbs), slow-cook or grill
- **Cured ham:** Traditional preservation (salt-cured, smoked)

**Butcher's Choice:**

**Option A: Ham Roasts**
- 4× Ham Roast (5-6 lbs each) @ 2-3 sp/lb = 10-18 sp each
- Total: 40-72 sp
- Use: Family meals, holidays, slow-cooker

**Option B: Ham Steaks (RECOMMENDED)**
- 16-18× Ham Steak (12-14 oz each) @ 2-2.5 sp each = 2-2.5 sp each
- Total: 32-45 sp
- Use: Quick meals, grilling, individual portions

**Retail Pricing:** Roasts = 2-3 sp/lb, Steaks = 2-2.5 sp each

---

### PRIMAL 7: SHANK (All 4 Legs)

**Location:** Lower legs (radius/ulna front, tibia/fibula rear)
**Weight:** 8-10% of dressed weight (9-12 lbs on 117 lb carcass, all 4 shanks combined)
**Character:** Very tough, collagen-rich, requires braising
**Fat Content:** Almost none (<1%)

**Processing:**
- **Front shanks:** Remove at knee joint (2 shanks, 2-3 lbs each)
- **Rear shanks:** Remove at hock joint (2 shanks, 2.5-3.5 lbs each)
- **Bone-in:** Always leave bone in (marrow, structure for braising)

**Best Uses:**
- **Braised shanks:** Osso buco style (slow-cook 3-4 hours, fall-off-bone tender)
- **Soup bones:** Marrow, stock, collagen (gelatin-rich broth)
- **Stew meat:** If deboned (very tough, requires long cooking)

**Retail Cuts:**
- 4× Shank (bone-in, 2-3.5 lbs each) @ 1.5-2 sp each = 1.5-2 sp each
- Total: 6-8 sp

**Retail Pricing:** 1.5-2 sp each (utility, but delicious when braised)

## V. NO FAT RENDERING (Why Deer Are Different from Bear/Pork)

### The Math: Why We Don't Render Deer Fat

**Bear Fat Rendering Economics (300 lb Black Bear):**
- Total fat trim: 30 lbs (10% body weight, fall condition)
- Rendered bear grease: 25 lbs (83% yield)
- Value: 25 lbs × 5 sp/lb = **125 sp** (12.5 gp)
- Labor: 8 hours × 4 sp/hour = 32 sp
- **Net profit: 93 sp** (9.3 gp) from fat alone
- **Fat = 35% of total bear value**

**Deer Fat Rendering Economics (180 lb Whitetail):**
- Total fat trim: 5 lbs (2-3% body weight, fall condition)
- Rendered deer fat: 4 lbs (80% yield, similar to bear)
- Value: 4 lbs × 3-4 sp/lb = **12-16 sp** (1.2-1.6 gp)
- Labor: 6 hours × 4 sp/hour = 24 sp
- **Net profit: -12 to -8 sp** (LOSS of 0.8-1.2 gp)
- **Fat = NEGATIVE value** (labor costs more than product worth)

**Conclusion:** **DO NOT render deer fat.** Trim it, discard it, or feed it to dogs. Not economically viable.

**Exception:** Large elk/moose (600-1,400 lbs) yield 10-20 lbs fat trim. Still not worth rendering (10-20 lbs × 3 sp = 30-60 sp revenue, 6-8 hours labor = 24-32 sp cost = marginal 6-28 sp profit). Only worth it if you're doing it yourself (zero labor cost).

## VI. FIELD DRESSING PROCEDURE (Wilderness Processing)

### Why Field Dressing Matters

**Immediate field dressing (within 30-60 minutes of kill) is CRITICAL for venison quality:**
- **Blood drainage:** Prevents "blood shot" meat (off-flavor, dark color, reduced value -20-30%)
- **Cooling:** Removes organs (heat generators), allows carcass to cool
- **Spoilage prevention:** Bacteria multiply in warm organs (gut bacteria contaminate meat if delayed)
- **Weight reduction:** 35% lighter carcass (easier to drag/carry out of field)

**Consequences of Delayed Field Dressing:**
- **0-30 minutes:** Ideal (no quality loss)
- **30-60 minutes:** Acceptable (minimal quality impact, warm weather risky)
- **1-2 hours:** Poor (meat quality degraded -10-15%, off-flavor risk)
- **2+ hours:** Spoiled (bacteria contamination, unsafe to eat in warm weather 60°F+)

---

### Field Dressing Step-by-Step

**Equipment Needed:**
- Sharp knife (4-6 inch blade, gut hook optional)
- Rope (50 ft, for hanging/dragging)
- Clean cloth/paper towels (wipe cavity)
- Plastic bags (heart, liver if keeping)
- Gloves (optional, recommended for hygiene)

**STEP 1: Position Deer**
- Lay deer on back, head uphill if on slope (gravity helps drainage)
- Spread hind legs (expose belly)

**STEP 2: Make Initial Cut**
- Start at sternum (breastbone), cut skin only (shallow, 1/2 inch deep)
- Cut down belly to pelvic bone (avoid cutting intestines)
- Use two fingers to lift skin away from organs (prevent puncturing stomach/intestines)

**STEP 3: Open Body Cavity**
- Cut through abdominal muscle (follow initial skin cut)
- **CRITICAL:** Do NOT puncture stomach, intestines, bladder (contaminates meat)
- Cut around anus (free intestines from pelvic canal)

**STEP 4: Remove Organs**
- Reach into chest cavity, cut diaphragm (separates chest from abdomen)
- Cut windpipe/esophagus at base of throat
- **Pull organs out:** Everything comes out together (heart, lungs, liver, stomach, intestines)
- Tip deer to drain blood (pour out of body cavity)

**STEP 5: Save Edible Organs (Optional)**
- **Heart:** 0.5-1 lb (good eating, 1-2 sp value)
- **Liver:** 1.5-3 lbs (if undamaged by shot, 2-4 sp value)
- Place in plastic bags, keep cool

**STEP 6: Wipe Cavity Clean**
- Use cloth to wipe out blood, debris
- **DO NOT rinse with water** (traps moisture, promotes bacteria)
- Prop cavity open with stick (air circulation, cooling)

**STEP 7: Cool & Transport**
- Hang deer (head up or down, air circulation)
- Cool to 40-50°F within 4-6 hours (critical for meat quality)
- Transport to processing location

**Time Required:** 15-30 minutes (experienced), 30-60 minutes (beginner)

---

### Field Dressing Survival DCs (D&D 5e)

**DC 8:** Common whitetail, favorable conditions (cool weather, daytime, good light)
**DC 10:** Standard conditions (moderate weather, forest, adequate light)
**DC 12:** Difficult conditions (large deer 200+ lbs, rain, poor light, heat)
**DC 15:** Large game (elk/moose 600+ lbs, heavy animal, remote location)
**DC 18:** Emergency conditions (wounded hunter, darkness, extreme weather, massive moose)

**Failure Consequences:**
- **Fail by 1-4:** Meat quality -10% (minor blood shot, slight off-flavor)
- **Fail by 5-9:** Meat quality -20% (significant contamination, organ puncture)
- **Fail by 10+:** Meat ruined (intestine puncture, spoilage, unsafe to eat)

## VII. AGING VENISON (Critical for Lean Meat)

### Why Aging Matters for Deer (More Than Bear/Pork)

**Deer meat is LEAN (2-3% fat).** Without aging, venison is TOUGH and GAMEY.

**Aging Process:**
- **Enzymatic breakdown:** Natural enzymes in meat break down muscle fibers (tenderizing)
- **Moisture loss:** Concentrates flavor, firms texture
- **Flavor development:** "Gamey" flavor mellows, becomes more pleasant

**Bear/Pork Don't Need Aging:**
- **Fat content:** 8-25% fat = natural tenderness (marbling, moisture)
- **Domestic pork:** Bred for tenderness (vs wild deer tough muscle)
- **Bear meat:** Fatty enough to mask toughness

**Deer REQUIRES Aging:**
- **Lean muscle:** No fat to provide moisture/tenderness
- **Wild game:** Tough, well-exercised muscle (vs domestic lazy cattle)
- **Aging = mandatory** (skip aging = tough, disappointing venison)

---

### Dry Aging Procedure

**Conditions:**
- **Temperature:** 38-42°F (refrigerator or cool cellar)
- **Humidity:** 60-70% (prevents excessive drying)
- **Air circulation:** Fan or open air (prevents mold)
- **Duration:** 7-14 days (longer = more tender, but more moisture loss)

**Process:**
1. Hang whole carcass (or halves) in cool room
2. Leave hide OFF (removed during field dressing)
3. Air circulates around carcass (dries surface, forms "pellicle")
4. Enzymes work inside meat (tenderizing)
5. After 7-14 days, fabricate into retail cuts

**Moisture Loss:**
- 7 days: 5-7% weight loss
- 14 days: 8-12% weight loss
- **Example:** 117 lb dressed carcass → 103-109 lbs after 14-day aging

**Why Remove Hide Before Aging:**
- **Deer hide is thin** (unlike cattle thick hide that protects during aging)
- **Hide traps moisture** (promotes bacterial growth, spoilage)
- **Air must contact meat** (surface drying = pellicle formation)

## VIII. VENISON PRESERVATION (Lean Meat Focus)

### Challenge: Preserving Lean Meat (No Fat to Protect)

**Fat = Moisture and Flavor:**
- Bear/Pork: 8-25% fat (retains moisture during smoking/drying)
- Deer: 2-3% fat (dries out easily, becomes tough/dry if over-processed)

**Venison-Specific Techniques:**

**1. BRINING (Moisture Retention)**
- Soak venison in salt/sugar/water solution (8-24 hours before smoking)
- Brine penetrates muscle (retains moisture during smoking)
- **Formula:** 1 gallon water, 3/4 cup salt, 1/2 cup sugar, spices
- Result: Smoked venison stays moist (vs dry/tough if not brined)

**2. ADDING FAT (Sausage/Ground Venison)**
- Mix 80% venison + 20% pork fat (ground together)
- Result: Juicy burgers/sausages (pure venison = dry, crumbly)
- **Example:** 10 lbs venison + 2.5 lbs pork fat = 12.5 lbs sausage mix

**3. SHORT SMOKING TIMES (Prevent Over-Drying)**
- Venison: 4-6 hours (vs pork 8-12 hours)
- Lower temps: 180-200°F (vs pork 225-250°F)
- Result: Smoky flavor without drying out meat

**4. JERKY (Ideal for Lean Meat)**
- Lean meat = perfect jerky (no fat to turn rancid)
- Slice thin (1/4 inch), marinate, dry
- Result: Shelf-stable (6-12 months), lightweight, nutritious

## IX. BUTCHER'S CHOICE SUMMARY

### Recommended Cutting Options by Cut Type

**BACKSTRAP (Premium, Highest Value):**
- **Steaks = BEST** (individual servings, quick sale, highest $/lb)
- Whole backstraps = Special orders only (hard to sell, ties up inventory)

**RIB (Premium):**
- **Chops = GOOD** (individual servings, grilling, steady demand)
- Rib roasts = Holiday/special occasion (slower sales, premium buyers)

**HAM (Large Volume):**
- **Steaks = BEST** (portion control, faster sales, versatile)
- Roasts = Family size (slower sales, fewer buyers need 5-6 lb roasts)

**SHOULDER:**
- **Ground = BEST** (versatile, high demand, uses tough cuts profitably)
- Roasts = Slow-cooker crowd (moderate demand)

**SHANK:**
- **Bone-in shanks ONLY** (no choice - structure needed for braising)

## X. BASELINE PROCESSING TIMELINE

### Time Estimates by Deer Size

**Small Deer (40-80 lbs, Roe Deer):**
- Field dressing: 15-20 minutes
- Skinning: 20-30 minutes
- Aging: 7 days
- Fabrication: 2-3 hours
- **Total Active Time: 3-4 hours**

**Medium Deer (120-200 lbs, Whitetail):**
- Field dressing: 20-30 minutes
- Skinning: 30-45 minutes
- Aging: 7-10 days
- Fabrication: 4-6 hours
- **Total Active Time: 5-7 hours**

**Large Deer (400-700 lbs, Elk):**
- Field dressing: 45-90 minutes
- Skinning: 1.5-2 hours
- Aging: 10-14 days
- Fabrication: 8-12 hours
- **Total Active Time: 11-15 hours**

**Massive Deer (900-2,200 lbs, Moose/Giant Elk):**
- Field dressing: 1.5-3 hours (requires 2-3 people)
- Skinning: 2-4 hours
- Aging: 14 days
- Fabrication: 14-20 hours
- **Total Active Time: 18-27 hours** (multi-day project)
